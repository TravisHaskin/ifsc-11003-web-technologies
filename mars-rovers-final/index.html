Exploring the Red Planet

Welcome to the Red Planet: Exploring Mars with NASA's Rovers

Mars is no place for the faint-hearted. It’s dry, rocky, and bitter cold. The fourth planet from the Sun, Mars is one of Earth's two closest planetary neighbors (Venus is the other). Mars is one of the easiest planets to spot in the night sky — it looks like a bright red point of light.

Despite being inhospitable to humans, robotic explorers — like NASA's Perseverance rover and Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter — serve as pathfinders to eventually get astronauts to the surface of the Red Planet.

NASA is reimagining the future of Mars exploration, driving new scientific discoveries, and preparing for humans on Mars. NASA’s Mars Exploration Program will focus the next two decades on its science-driven systemic approach on these strategic goals: exploring for potential life, understanding the geology and climate of Mars, and preparation for human exploration.

Meet the Rovers

Each robotic explorer sent to the Red Planet has its own unique capabilities driven by science. Many attributes of a rover take on human-like features, such as “heads,” “bodies,” and “arms and legs."

Curiosity

  • Mission Goal:
    • Determine if Mars ever had the environmental conditions to support microbial life.
  • Launch:
    • November 26, 2011
  • Landing:
    • August 6, 2012
  • Key Discoveries:
    • Found chemical and mineral evidence that Gale Crater was once a calm lakebed and was habitable in the ancient past.
    • Found organic molecules (carbon-containing chemical building blocks of life) preserved in rock layers.
    • Characterizing surface radiation.
  • Key Technical Features:
    • Large size (about the size of a small SUV)
    • Multi-Mission Radioisotope Thermoelectric generator (MMRTG) power source (uses heat from plutonium decay)
    • Sky Crane landing system
    • Rock-vaporizing laser (ChemCam)
    • Drill for collecting rock samples
      • Extensive onboard science laboratory
  • Status:
    • Active (Ongoing extended mission)

Opportunity

  • Mission Goal:
    • Search for and characterize rocks and soils that hold clues to past water activity on Mars, specifically searching for evidence of ancient standing water.
  • Launch:
    • July 7, 2003
  • Landing:
    • January 24, 2004
  • Key Discoveries:
    • Found definitive proof of liquid water on ancient Mars
    • Set the off-world driving record
  • Key Technical Features:
    • Solar-powered(designed for 90 sols/days, lasted nearly 15 years)
    • Rock Abrasian Tool (RAT) to grind away rock surfaces
    • Panoramic Camera (Pancam)
    • Rocker-bogie suspension system
  • Status:
    • Mission Complete (Last communicated June 10, 2018, following a planet-wide dust storm)

Spirit

  • Mission Goal:
    • Search for and characterize rocks and soils that hold clues to past water activity on Mars, complementing the Opportunity mission.
  • Launch:
    • June 10, 2003
  • Landing:
    • January 3, 2004
  • Key Discoveries:
    • Found evidence that Mars was once much wetter than it is today, including the discovery of carbonates and hematite suggesting past water activity, and evidence of ancient hydrothermal systems
  • Key Technical Features:
    • Identical twin to Opportunity
    • Solar power
    • Rock Abrasion Tool (RAT)
    • Pancam
    • Rocker-boogie suspension system
  • Status:
    • Mission Complete (Became embedded in soft soil in 2009; ceased transmitting in March 2010)
